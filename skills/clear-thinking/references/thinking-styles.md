# Thinking Styles

A menu of lenses for the core process (SKILL.md, step 7). The linear process handles most of the work; these catch what a single pass misses. **Don't run all of them.** Pick the 2–3 that match the decision — the "use when" line tells you which. Each has a prompt you can run directly.

A lens earns its place only if it changes the answer or exposes something the core steps missed. If it just restates the recommendation, drop it.

---

## Inversion

Instead of "how do I make this succeed," ask how it would guarantee *failure* — then avoid those paths.

- **Use when:** the bet is risky or hard to reverse, or the upside case feels too clean.
- **Prompt:** "If I wanted this decision to fail catastrophically, what would I do? Am I doing any of it?"
- **Catches:** failure modes that optimism hides. Often more actionable than chasing the win.

## Pre-mortem

Assume the decision already failed, then explain why — *before* committing.

- **Use when:** any consequential decision, especially with unknown unknowns (this is the tool for step 6's blind-spot hunt).
- **Prompt:** "It's a year from now and this was clearly the wrong call. Write the postmortem. What was the reason?"
- **Catches:** the risk everyone felt but no one said out loud. Named risks can be planned around; unnamed ones do the damage.

## Second-order thinking

Play consequences forward more than one step. "And then what? And then what?"

- **Use when:** the first-order outcome is obviously good — that's exactly when the second-order cost bites (incentives shift, people adapt, the easy win creates a dependency).
- **Prompt:** "This works as intended. What does it cause next? And after that?"
- **Catches:** solutions that fix today and create tomorrow's problem.

## First-principles

Strip the problem to what must physically/economically be true, and rebuild — instead of reasoning by analogy to what others do.

- **Use when:** the default answer is "because that's how it's done," or costs/constraints are taken as fixed when they might not be.
- **Prompt:** "Forget how this is normally solved. What is actually, unavoidably true here? What would I build up from those facts alone?"
- **Catches:** inherited assumptions masquerading as laws of nature.

## Base rates (the outside view)

Before trusting your specific story, ask how situations *like this one* usually turn out.

- **Use when:** forecasting, estimating timelines/costs, or judging odds of success. Especially when you feel "this time is different."
- **Prompt:** "Ignore the details of my case. Historically, how do projects/bets/timelines like this go? Start from that number, then adjust."
- **Catches:** the planning fallacy and inside-view optimism. Your estimate is usually the exception you wish were true.

## Expected value

Weight each outcome by its probability, not by how vivid or scary it feels.

- **Use when:** outcomes vary widely, or fear/excitement is distorting a probability.
- **Prompt:** "List the outcomes, rough probability of each, and payoff of each. What's the probability-weighted value — and does a low-odds catastrophe dominate it?"
- **Catches:** decisions driven by a salient outcome (a dramatic downside, a jackpot upside) that's actually unlikely. Also flags ruin risk — a bet with positive EV but a small chance of unrecoverable loss is still a bad bet.

## Reversibility (one-way vs. two-way doors)

Sort the decision by whether it can be cheaply undone.

- **Use when:** always available as a tiebreaker; decisive when options are close.
- **Prompt:** "Is this a one-way door (expensive/impossible to reverse) or a two-way door (walk it back cheaply)? Two-way doors deserve speed and less agonizing; one-way doors deserve the full process."
- **Catches:** over-deliberating on reversible calls, and under-deliberating on irreversible ones.

## Pareto (the vital few)

Most of the payoff comes from a small share of the inputs. Find that share, do it, and consciously name what you're *not* doing.

- **Use when:** the work could sprawl — a long to-do list, "improve everything," many possible fixes competing for limited time.
- **Prompt:** "Of everything I could do here, which few moves carry most of the result? Name them, then say out loud what I'm deliberately skipping and why that's fine."
- **Catches:** effort spread thin across low-impact work. The real output isn't the list of what to do — it's the shorter list, plus permission to ignore the rest.

## Choosing lenses by problem type

| The decision is about… | Reach for |
|---|---|
| A risky, hard-to-undo bet | Inversion, pre-mortem, reversibility |
| A forecast or estimate | Base rates, expected value |
| "Everyone does it this way" | First-principles |
| A clean, obvious-looking win | Second-order, pre-mortem |
| Choosing under real uncertainty | Expected value, base rates |
| A fast, low-stakes call | Reversibility (and little else — don't over-think a two-way door) |
| "Improve everything" / a sprawling to-do list | Pareto, first-principles |
